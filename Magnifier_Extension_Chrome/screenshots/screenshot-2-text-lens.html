Optical Illusions and the Science of Human Vision

Last edited March 28, 2026  ·  Wikipedia contributors

An optical illusion is a phenomenon that occurs when the information gathered by the eye is processed by the brain in a way that produces a false or misleading image that differs from reality. Visual illusions exploit the complex predictive mechanisms that the visual cortex uses to make sense of the world.

The human visual system relies on pattern recognition, contrast enhancement, and motion estimation to interpret visual scenes. When these heuristics are applied to carefully constructed stimuli, they can produce compelling perceptual errors that are consistent across populations regardless of age or cultural background.

Common categories include geometric illusions, ambiguous figures, paradox illusions, and colour-contrast effects. The Müller-Lyer illusion, for example, causes equal-length lines to appear different in size due to the orientation of arrowheads at each end.
