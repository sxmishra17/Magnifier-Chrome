Neural Adaptation in Low-Vision Assistive Systems:
A Longitudinal Study
Dr. Elena Vasquez¹ · Prof. James Okoye² · Dr. Yuki Tanaka¹
¹Institute for Accessibility Research, MIT  ·  ²UCL Centre for Vision Science
Abstract
We present findings from a 24-month longitudinal study examining neural adaptation in users of digital low-vision aids across three assistive modalities: screen magnification, optical character recognition with audio feedback, and contrast-enhancement overlays. Participants (n=147) exhibited statistically significant improvements in reading comprehension scores (p<0.001) after 12 weeks of consistent magnifier use, with cortical reorganisation observed via fMRI in 68% of subjects. These findings suggest that lightweight, hover-based magnification tools represent a clinically meaningful intervention for acquired low vision.
Keywords: low vision, digital magnification, neural plasticity, assistive technology, accessibility
1. Introduction
Low vision, defined as visual acuity of less than 6/18 in the better eye even with correction, affects an estimated 246 million people worldwide (WHO, 2023). While traditional optical aids such as handheld magnifiers and stand magnifiers remain in widespread use, digital screen-based magnification has grown rapidly as a first-line intervention due to its low cost and accessibility.
Unlike full-page zoom, hover-based magnification preserves the spatial context of surrounding content, reducing the cognitive load associated with navigation. Prior work by Chen et al. 2. Methods
Participants were recruited from ophthalmology clinics across three metropolitan areas. Inclusion criteria required a confirmed diagnosis of acquired low vision with visual acuity between 6/24 and 6/60. Exclusion criteria included progressive retinal disease diagnosed within the preceding 6 months and prior use of screen magnification software exceeding 40 hours cumulative.
